About THREAD

An educational project revealing the true environmental cost of fast fashion.

Our Mission

THREAD is designed to look like the fast fashion websites you're used to‚Äîbut with one crucial difference. We show you what those other sites never will: the true environmental cost of every item.

When you browse H&M, Zara, Shein, or any other fast fashion retailer, you see price tags that seem incredibly low. $12.99 for a t-shirt. $29.99 for jeans. These prices seem like great deals‚Äîuntil you understand what they're really costing our planet.

The fashion industry is one of the world's largest polluters, responsible for 10% of global carbon emissions, 20% of global wastewater, and 92 million tons of textile waste each year. Yet this impact is invisible when we shop. THREAD makes it visible.

By presenting environmental data in a familiar shopping format, we hope to change how you see fast fashion forever. Our goal isn't to shame anyone‚Äîit's to inform, educate, and empower you to make choices that align with your values.

How We Calculate Impact

Our data comes from peer-reviewed research and industry reports

CO‚ÇÇ Emissions

We calculate carbon footprint based on life cycle assessments (LCAs) that account for raw material extraction, manufacturing, transportation, use phase, and end-of-life disposal. Our figures represent average emissions for typical fast fashion production methods.

Water Usage

Water footprint calculations include "blue water" (freshwater used), "green water" (rainwater for crops), and "grey water" (water needed to dilute pollutants). Data comes from the Water Footprint Network and academic studies.

Chemical Pollution

We document known toxic chemicals used in textile production, including pesticides (for cotton), dyes, finishing agents, and waterproofing treatments. Many chemicals used in fashion are known carcinogens or endocrine disruptors.

Decomposition Time

Time to decompose varies significantly by material. Natural fibers like cotton may decompose in 1-5 years, while synthetic materials like polyester can persist for 200+ years, shedding microplastics throughout.
